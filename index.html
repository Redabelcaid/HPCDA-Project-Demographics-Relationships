Tools Used:

The visual analytics tool was developed in-house by the team. Stack:

  • DuckDB (MIT) — embedded columnar SQL engine; used for ingestion of CSV inputs into Parquet and for aggregation queries that produce the derived JSON consumed by the frontend.
  • Node.js + TypeScript — backend runtime (Express HTTP server, derive pipeline, ingestion script).
  • Vite + TypeScript — frontend bundler and dev server.
  • D3.js (ISC) — 2D visualisations: city map, dashboard panels, group×group matrix, scatter plots, Sankey diagram, histograms.
  • Three.js (MIT) — 3D heightmap with custom GLSL shader for animated friendship arcs.
  • Python + scipy.stats — used once, off-pipeline, for the formal statistical analysis (Kruskal-Wallis, χ², η²) reported in the supplementary group analysis.

Questions

1 — Demographics of Engagement, Ohio.

The 1,011 study participants form a demographically balanced population. Ages spread uniformly across the 18–60 range with no dominant cohort. The majority hold at least a high-school diploma; roughly 40% hold a bachelor's degree or higher, with the 25–34 cohort over-represented in graduate-degree holders. Joviality (a continuous wellbeing score) is concentrated in a narrow empirical band of 0.42–0.56 with city mean 0.49; we render it on the map with a sequential plasma colormap fitted to this range rather than a divergent palette, because 0.5 has no semantic meaning for joviality. Average monthly spending breaks down as Shelter $680, Recreation $398, Food $345, Education $49 — with Recreation and Food at near-parity, indicating meaningful disposable income. The town hosts ten self-reported interest groups (A–J) ranging in size from 83 (Group E) to 116 (Group J); these groups are well-balanced and the redacted topic labels prevent confirmation bias.

See PDF report, Section 1 (Q1) for full analysis with 5 figures.

2 — Social network patterns.

Ten patterns are characterised from social network structure, group membership, and joviality. Key findings: (1) Intra-group interaction dominates — every interest group interacts most heavily with itself, forming a clear diagonal in the group×group matrix. (2) Cross-group bridges exist between E↔F and G↔H. (3) Friend count strongly predicts joviality — residents with under 50 friends concentrate in the unhappy range (joviality <0.3); residents with over 200 friends are predominantly happy (>0.6). (4) The Group H paradox — largest group, highest interaction count, above-average income, yet lowest joviality. Quantity of contact does not equal quality. (5) Geographically unconstrained networks — friendship arcs span the full city; social groups do not correspond to residential clusters. (6) Workplaces are powerful social hubs — 65% of coworker pairs are friends (vs. 16% baseline for random pairs); top 10 employers show 100% pair cohesion. A formal Kruskal-Wallis test confirms that of all demographic, financial, and behavioural variables tested, only social connection count significantly differentiates the interest groups (H = 30.0, p = 0.0004, η² = 2.8%).

See PDF report, Section 2 (Q2) for all 10 patterns with 7 figures.

3 — Business base of Engagement.

The town hosts 253 employers serving 1,011 participants. The business base is structurally composed of small and medium-sized employers (1–3 jobs is the modal size, long tail to ~9 jobs at the largest firm). No single employer dominates. Wages rise monotonically with education requirement (Low → HS/College → Bachelors → Graduate), but inter-tier overlap is substantial: a high-paying HS/College role can out-earn a low-paying Bachelors role. Approximately 78% of jobs run a conventional Monday–Friday schedule; the remainder includes retail and service positions with weekend or evening hours. The most striking labour-market finding is the role of workplaces as social hubs: 65% of all coworker pairs in the city are friends in the social network, compared to a 16% baseline for random participant pairs — a four-fold elevation. Our per-employer detail panel surfaces wage range, education-mix of the workforce, household composition, commute distance, and coworker friendship density for any selected employer.

See PDF report, Section 3 (Q3) for full analysis with 3 figures.

4 — One-page summary for residents.

Engagement is a demographically even-keeled, mid-sized town of about 1,011 study participants, balanced across age and education. The strongest predictor of how happy residents reported feeling was not wage, education, or interest group — it was the number of friends they have. Workplaces are powerful social anchors: nearly two-thirds of coworker pairs are friends. Friendships in Engagement are not bounded by neighbourhood; the friendship arcs span the full city, indicating a genuinely connected community rather than isolated districts. The local economy is composed of small businesses (no anchor firm), with wages tracking education on average but with wide overlap between tiers. Two findings worth resident reflection: (i) Group H is the largest and most socially active group yet reports the lowest happiness — activity volume does not translate to wellbeing; (ii) smaller, tighter groups like Group E (83 members) report the highest happiness despite average or below-average earnings. Tight community appears to matter more than individual prosperity.

See PDF report, Section 4 (Q4) for full resident-facing summary.

Methodology and caveats — see the PDF report, Section 5. Key points: (i) the MC2 ParticipantStatusLogs (~5 GB of 5-minute snapshots) were deliberately not ingested, except for one bounded cross-sectional pass to compute mean financial balances; (ii) 131 participants disappear from the data after month 1 (survivorship bias acknowledged throughout); (iii) residential buildings with fewer than 3 sampled residents are de-emphasised in map views via reduced opacity and diagonal hatching.
